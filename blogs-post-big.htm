in. Part 1: Vision of the Internet Future

domainname.com/original_post

Disclaimer: We start a series of stories describing various issues and problems that are related for the most part to the Internet, and our vision of possible ways to solve them. Those will often be followed by previews of projects mentioned in the stories. Keep in mind: some of them might change, some will remain just our fantasy; at this moment we are a small team, but with a great desire to change this world for the better or at least a little bit.

Victor came home in an excellent mood – he scored another rarity for his collection of the past. Gently taking a small slim and transparent plastic box with a logo of a long gone company out of the package he curiously turned it over in his hands. Full of excitement he opened it and took it to a brightly let window area to have a better look at its contents. The disc had “CD-R 650 MB” written on it. He released the disc from the case with a slight click. Victor turned it and saw barely visible multi-color sun reflections across the room created by the mirror backside of the disc.

- You got it!

Victor’s younger brother rushed into the room as a whirlwind.

- Give it to me! Let me see it!

He snatched the a bright item out of his brother’s hands while paying no attention to Victor’s discontent with such an arrogant handling of the rarity. He then brought the disc closer to his eyes.

- Hum… Is it only 650 Mb? Well, they didn’t really save on physical space in the past, if they wrote all of their data on such large size media with such small capacity. Just imagine what how many of these will be required to record at least one holographic movie!

His brother chuckled, however didn’t start to lecture once again about the past formats and how much space they took. Instead he sat at the table-screen, entered a password on its surface and opened his private “in” page. Few would use such this term, it was a unified entry point for everyone and as such it was meaningless to anybody’s identify. It was generally called “network”. Victor did read that not so long ago the network was divided into separate fragments called “web sites” and one needed to know their addresses to access them. They could be found through special services called search engines, a rather popular feature at that time. He even knew the name of one of it but it was odd and always skipped his memory. At some time later new projects appeared.

Their authors approached the problem from the other side and interchanged the core of the things – now information was looking for the user not the other way around. Victor was young and has just started to work in web development, he did however has heard from his more experienced workmates that earlier instead of developing logic and functionality of projects and filling them with data, people had to think about such things like separate design and UI for each (!) site. He always felt sorry for users who had to understand every time what and where was located and at the same time they had to be content with what the owner of the resource felt like adding. Or on another hand the user had to endure unnecessary elements of the forced UI. At the same time developers had to face an insidious beast whose name was cross-browser compatibility! Victor did not fully understand what the problem was, but the expressions of those telling the story gave him an idea about the immensity of the issue. Then phrases like “so that it would be displayed smoothly on the whole, $#@!, zoo of devices!“ brought to his imagination terrible monsters that the web-developers fought against like knights in glittering armor. Victor liked reading about knights.

While he did not know what “caching” meant he knew that in those days the connection was slow and at the same time one had to download a lot of junk needed to correctly display each separate web-site and besides much of that mess was duplicated many times! However, he tried to be not naive and did not believe every single word he was told: it often seemed to him that seniors simply pulled his leg and laughed behind his back. He listened to the stories about attacks referred to with dreadful words DDoS, or XSS, SQL with a lot of interest. For some reason he imagined virtual ancient pirate like battles. It was probably because the word “pirates” was also mentioned from time to time. But later he still had to ask more detailed questions about the subject. And even if this was true many such attacks couldn’t happen today as the system simply receives data textually (pure data) and sends is in a such manner as well. So by default this data cannot harm neither client nor server. Furthermore, this approach enables instant sending of small kilobyte pages since the client or server receives only useful data without any HTML, JS and CSS junk.

He also knew that earlier web pages were detached from the global processing. This task was to a very limited extent solved by the search engines he thought of earlier. He imagined them as the giants with clay feet. Victor liked looking for colorful analogies. Millions bowed down to them every day to get an answer regarding the network location of the required information and pity on you if you don’t know how to ask correctly! Also he heard some blood curdling stories about spam and non-relevant ads all over the place. The rumor has it that it was practically on every site. Victor found it rather useful that now the network offered custom ads at the appropriate moment based on his preferences and requirements but he couldn’t understand what good it was to show the same canned ads to everyone. Authors of articles, books, music, and video, manufacturers of various products – all of them had to participate in the insane rush for consumers. And then these consumers also had to look for the necessary information and products. An incredible amount of money was spent for ads and that increased the prices not to mention that these ads were almost never objective and simply deceived consumers or at best emiftished the goods.

The story was that at that time the pieces of network called websites were not able to distinguish users and knew nothing about their interests. Once again Victor felt sorry for the users. He pictured them as unfortunate small creatures wandering in a huge labyrinth, trying to force their way to the required information through the wilderness of ads and spam that grew in abundance as enormous trees and tried to catch users with their nasty branches. And even if someone managed to avoid them, it was too early to rejoice: one had to mind further steps as not to be trapped by fraudulent websites that were spread all over the network. The Trust Level concept was not used at that time as a universal tool that got rid of fakes and spam once and for all.

However social networks were the craftiest! Many users didn’t even suspect or simply didn’t listen to people warning about the risk of providing personal data to such resources. Victor just shook his head in amazement and disbelief when he heard stories about such networks. Now every user had an individual reliable Vault-server that securely safeguarded the owner’s data. The theft of personal data is severely punished but to think that someone was to give it to others on their own?? No, apparently these were just tall tales or at least greatly exaggerated stories like many other things told him by his senior colleagues about the Internet past. The only thing that he didn’t doubt was that all user data was saved not on Vault-servers but locally or on discs like the one he held in this hands – a piece of the past sparkling in the sun rays!
